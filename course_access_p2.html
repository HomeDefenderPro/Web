Module 2.1: Entry Point Hardening: Doors and Frames

Approximately 70% of home invasions occur through the front or back door. This module focuses on turning your door, frame, and hardware into a system designed to resist **kick-in violence** and blunt force attack.

The Weakest Link: The frame, not the door, is the point of failure. The common weak point is the strike plate, secured by tiny 1-inch screws only into the thin wood of the door jamb. Our focus is anchoring this critical point directly to the structural studs behind the frame.

Deadbolt and Strike Plate Reinforcement

The standard residential strike plate is designed for function, not security. We must upgrade this layer:

  • High-Security Deadbolts: Use deadbolts with a 1-inch minimum throw and a free-spinning hardened steel cylinder guard to resist drilling and wrenching. **Always use Grade 1 commercial-rated hardware.**
  • Extended Strike Plates: Replace the standard plate with a **heavy-gauge, extended strike plate** (at least 12-18 inches long). This plate must be secured by **3-inch or 4-inch screws** that penetrate through the door jamb and deep into the structural framing (the king and jack studs). This physically anchors the locking point to the house structure.

Securing the Hinge Side (The Push Side)

Intruders often target the hinge side on doors that swing outwards, as pins can be removed. On inward-swinging doors, the hinge side still requires reinforcement to resist warping or splitting from a kick-in attack.

  • **Hinge Reinforcement:** Every hinge must be secured with at least **two 3-inch or 4-inch screws** that penetrate the jamb and anchor into the nearest stud. This prevents the door from flexing and splitting the frame during a violent attack.
  • **Non-Removable Pin Hinges (Outswing Doors):** For doors that swing out (common in high-wind areas), replace standard hinges with non-removable pin hinges or set screws to prevent pin removal.

Module Takeaways 2.1

Core Principle: The single most effective action to prevent a kick-in is installing an **extended strike plate** secured with **3-inch or 4-inch screws** that penetrate the door frame and anchor into the structural studs.

Module 2.2: Window & Non-Traditional Entry Defense

Non-traditional entry points—windows, sliding doors, and the garage—are often overlooked and exploited by criminals. This module focuses on low-cost, high-impact reinforcement techniques for these secondary breaches.

Securing Sliding Glass Doors and Non-Standard Openings

Sliding doors fail in two ways: the lock is flimsy, or the door is lifted off its track. Both must be countered.

  1. **Vertical Barricade:** Use a cut-to-fit **solid dowel or metal bar** placed in the bottom track to prevent the door from sliding open, regardless of the lock quality.
  2. **Track and Lift Denial:** Install screws or rivets at the top of the track, leaving just enough space for the door to slide freely. This prevents the door from being lifted off its track from the outside.
  3. **Window Film:** Apply **security window film** (minimum 8-mil thickness) to the interior side of large, vulnerable glass panels (like sliding doors or large picture windows). This film does not prevent the glass from breaking, but it holds the shattered pieces together, forcing the intruder to spend critical minutes tearing through the plastic-like barrier.

Basement and Garage Defense

The door between the garage and the main house is often a standard, low-security interior door. Once the garage is breached, this door is the final barrier.

  • **Garage-to-Home Door Upgrade:** This door must be treated as an exterior door. It should be a **solid core or metal door** equipped with a Grade 1 deadbolt and secured with the same 3-inch/4-inch strike plate screws detailed in Module 2.1.
  • **Basement Windows:** These are small but accessible. They should be protected by metal security grates or highly durable polycarbonate sheets fitted internally. Ensure the grates or sheets can be quickly removed from the inside in case of fire (egress compliance).

Module Takeaways 2.2

Core Principle: The door between the garage and the home should be secured to the **same Grade 1 standard** as any exterior entry point, as the garage offers an intruder valuable time and cover.

Module 2.3: Smart Security and Surveillance Optimization

Technical security systems—cameras, alarms, and sensors—are your active, electronic perimeter. Their effectiveness relies entirely on correct placement, reliability, and proper integration with the physical environment.

Optimal Camera Placement and Coverage

Your cameras must serve two functions: deterrence and forensic capture. They must cover points of approach and entry while being difficult to disable.

  1. **Height and Angle:** Mount cameras high (**9-12 feet**) and angle them downward. This provides a wide field of view and makes them difficult for a trespasser to reach or obscure. **Avoid pointing cameras directly at bright light sources** (sun, porch lights) which can wash out the image.
  2. **Targeted Coverage:** The camera's primary focus should be the **face of the individual** approaching the door or window. Don't waste coverage on broad property lines unless necessary for the perimeter. Ensure overlapping coverage to eliminate dead zones.
  3. **Power Redundancy:** Use cameras with battery backup or PoE (Power over Ethernet) to ensure they remain active if the main power is cut during a breach attempt.

Self-Monitored Alarm System Best Practices

A self-monitored system (like Ring, SimpliSafe, etc.) requires a high level of personal vigilance. The most critical element is reducing the time between detection and your response (or the pre-programmed automated response).

  • **Layered Sensor Placement:** Utilize both door/window sensors and **internal motion sensors**. Place motion sensors in high-traffic areas (halls, stairwells) to confirm an interior breach, reducing false alarms from a door opening briefly.
  • **Panic Integration:** Ensure key access points (like the primary bedroom) have immediate access to a **physical or app-based panic button** that triggers the loudest possible siren and immediately notifies designated contacts/authorities.
  • **Integration with Lighting:** Program the alarm system to **flash all exterior and interior lights** immediately upon triggering. This disorients the intruder, identifies the location to neighbors, and provides light for cameras.

Module Takeaways 2.3

Core Principle: Cameras should be mounted **high (9-12 feet)** and their primary function is to secure forensic evidence by capturing the **face of the individual** approaching the entry point.
