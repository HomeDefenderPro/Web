Module 3.1: Immediate Action Plans (IAP) and Decision Matrix

An Immediate Action Plan is a scripted, rehearsed response designed to eliminate decision-making under stress. It is the tactical roadmap for what you do the second the alarm sounds or you hear an intrusion.

The OODA Loop Principle: Your IAP is designed to disrupt the intruder's OODA Loop (Observe, Orient, Decide, Act). By having your response pre-determined, you execute your action while the intruder is still observing and orienting, giving you the critical **time-on-target** advantage.

The Decision Matrix: Stay vs. Evacuate

Every IAP begins with a core decision matrix based on location, threat, and time. Your default response is to **Barricade and Call 911** from your safe room, unless a clear evacuation path is available.

  • Scenario 1: Noise in an Unoccupied Area: If the intrusion is detected far from occupied bedrooms (e.g., the garage or basement), the IAP is **Barricade and Deny**. Secure all occupied doors, establish communication with 911, and wait for law enforcement.
  • Scenario 2: Imminent Threat/Hallway Breach: If the intrusion is detected directly outside a bedroom, the IAP is **Loud Verbal Challenge and Defense**. Use noise and light to draw attention and confirm intent before taking physical defensive action.
  • Scenario 3: Confirmed Fire/Emergency Exit: If the primary threat is fire or other non-intruder emergency, the IAP prioritizes **Immediate Evacuation** to the rally point. Security plans must be subordinate to life safety plans.

The Rally Point and Family Communication

A successful IAP relies on instant family synchronization.

  • Internal Communication: Establish a code word (e.g., "Code Red") that immediately signifies an intrusion and cues the family to execute the IAP without question. This is more effective than shouting "Intruder!"
  • External Rally Point (Safe Haven): Establish a well-lit, non-obvious location **outside the property perimeter** (e.g., a neighbor's house or corner store) where the family must gather after an evacuation. This prevents family members from re-entering the danger zone looking for others.
  • **Communication with Dispatch:** Practice the required information for a 911 call: **Location (address), Nature of the Emergency (intruder), Number of Intruders (if known), Your Location (safe room), and the Description of the Threat.**

Module 3.2: Safe Room Design and Barrier Tactics

The **Safe Room** is the final, hardened defensive position. It must be prepared to delay entry long enough for law enforcement to arrive, turning the room itself into a delaying obstacle.

Bedroom Door Fortification (The Barrier)

Unlike exterior doors, interior doors are hollow and weak. They cannot be reinforced with screws into the frame studs.

  • Security Barricade Device: Employ a purpose-built, internal door barricade system (e.g., a floor jammer, security bar, or door wedge) on the primary safe room door. These devices redirect force from the weak lock/frame to the **tensile strength of the floor** or wall, buying significant time (typically over 60 seconds).
  • Secondary Barriers: Stage heavy furniture (dressers, bookshelves) inside the safe room to be rapidly pushed against the door. This creates additional noise, visual obstruction, and a time-delay obstacle the intruder must defeat.
  • Hard-Points for Movement: During training, identify hard-points within the safe room that offer cover (e.g., behind a chimney, concrete wall) and strong angles for observation/defense, minimizing exposure to soft targets (drywall).

Tactical Staging and Gear Deployment

Your safe room must contain immediate-access gear to establish and maintain the advantage.

  • Communications: A dedicated, charged **cell phone** (separate from the primary) or a landline for 911 contact.
  • Light and Disorientation: A high-output **tactical flashlight** (minimum 500 lumens) to blind, disorient, and identify the threat in a dark hallway. Light is a force multiplier.
  • Defensive Tool Access: Any staged defensive tools (firearms, pepper spray, impact tools) must be secured but immediately accessible to the defender only (e.g., biometric safe). **Access time must be under 5 seconds.**
  • Medical Kit: A small, accessible IFAK (Individual First Aid Kit) staged to address severe bleeding until professional help arrives.

Module 3.3: Post-Incident Protocol and Maintaining Legal Integrity

The moments immediately following a defensive action are legally and emotionally critical. This protocol ensures you transition safely to the arrival of law enforcement and preserve the integrity of your legal defense.

Transitioning to Law Enforcement Arrival (The 'Contact' Phase)

This phase is high-risk. Law enforcement arrives under high stress and cannot distinguish the defender from the threat initially.

  1. Staging the Scene: If safe, secure your defensive tool and place it out of immediate reach (e.g., on a high shelf or secured on your person, muzzle down). You must be unarmed when making initial contact with police unless instructed otherwise.
  2. Identification and Instructions: Announce your identity loudly and clearly from the safe room when police arrive. Follow all verbal commands instantly and without argument.
  3. The 'Brief and Factual' Statement: When the initial officer asks what happened, limit your statement to a few facts: **"I was in fear for my life, they broke in, the threat is secured, I need medical assistance."** Do not provide a full, detailed narrative or answer complex questions at this time.

The Legal Integrity Protocol

Your actions immediately after the incident are key to establishing **self-defense immunity** in many jurisdictions.

  • Preserve the Scene: Do not clean, move, or touch anything unnecessarily. The state of the scene is forensic evidence of the threat you faced.
  • Invoke Your Rights: Politely inform officers: **"I respect your work, but I will not be answering any further questions without my attorney present."** This is your most critical legal defense move. Any statement made under duress or shock can be used against you.
  • Documenting the Threat: If safe and the immediate threat is contained, use your phone to capture photographic evidence of the breach (e.g., the kicked-in door, shattered window, signs of forced entry) *before* it is disturbed by forensic teams.

Module Takeaways 3.3

Core Principle: Upon law enforcement arrival, you must **secure your defensive tool** and make only a **Brief and Factual Statement**. The most important legal move is to politely **invoke your right to counsel** immediately after the initial safety check.
