Module 4.1: Digital Footprint Minimization (OPSEC) and Privacy Hardening

Operational Security (OPSEC) involves protecting sensitive personal data that criminals can use to plan an attack, steal your identity, or target your family's routine. This is the **Denial of Information** phase.

Data Triangulation: Criminals exploit three vectors: **Public Records** (property ownership, address), **Social Media** (routines, vacation timing, asset display), and **Breached Databases** (passwords, emails). Minimize data exposure in all three areas to eliminate planning resources.

Eliminating Public Records Exposure

Public-facing data often confirms your current residence, travel habits, and financial capacity.

  • Data Broker Opt-Out: Use dedicated services or manually contact the top 10 **People Search Websites** (data brokers like Whitepages, Spokeo) to remove your name, address, and phone number. This must be an ongoing, quarterly action.
  • Property Ownership Masking: For advanced security, consult with an attorney to transfer property ownership to a **Trust or LLC** to prevent a simple search from revealing your personal name and address in public land records.
  • Mail Security: Never leave mail in an unlocked box for extended periods. Consider using a P.O. Box for all non-essential correspondence.

Social Media OPSEC Protocol

Social media is the single greatest source of intelligence for criminals planning a non-random attack.

  • Privacy Lockdown: Set all primary social media accounts to the **highest privacy settings** ("Friends Only" or equivalent). Disable geotagging/location services for all photo uploads.
  • The 48-Hour Rule: Never post vacation photos, travel check-ins, or high-value asset photos (cars, unique jewelry, high-end electronics) in real-time. Post these updates with a **minimum 48-hour delay** to obscure the current occupancy status of your home.
  • Children's Privacy: Never post specific school names, sports schedules, or location-based photos of children. This exposes their routine and potential attack vectors.

Module Takeaways 4.1

Core Principle: Minimize data triangulation by performing **quarterly data broker opt-outs** and strictly observing the **48-Hour Rule** for all social media posts related to home occupancy or travel.

Module 4.2: Network Hardening and Remote Access Security

Your home network is the gateway to your cameras, smart locks, computers, and financial data. A compromised network defeats your technical physical defenses.

Router and Network Configuration Hardening

The foundation of digital defense is a secure Wi-Fi network that prevents unauthorized access.

  • Change Default Credentials: Immediately change the router's **default admin password and username**. Default credentials are listed publicly and are the easiest entry point for hackers.
  • Disable WPS and UPnP: Disable Wi-Fi Protected Setup (WPS) and Universal Plug and Play (UPnP) functions on your router. Both protocols are known security liabilities that offer easy remote access to the network.
  • Network Segmentation (The IoT Gap): Create a **separate, dedicated Guest or IoT (Internet of Things) network** for all smart devices (cameras, thermostats, smart TVs, lights). This ensures that if a vulnerable smart device is hacked, the attacker cannot pivot to your primary network containing sensitive computers and financial data.

VPN and Remote Access Protocol

How you access your home network or internet services while traveling must be secured.

  • Virtual Private Network (VPN) Use: Use a reputable, paid **VPN service** for all internet activity when connecting via public Wi-Fi (airports, hotels, coffee shops). This encrypts your data and prevents session hijacking.
  • Two-Factor Authentication (2FA) Mandate: Implement **2FA** on every account that supports it (banking, email, smart home systems, cameras). Use a dedicated authenticator app (e.g., Google Authenticator, Authy) instead of relying on SMS text messages.
  • Secure Firmware/Software: Keep all router firmware, operating systems, and smart home apps updated. Patches often fix critical security flaws exploited by attackers.

Module Takeaways 4.2

Core Principle: The single most critical network step is **Network Segmentation**—creating a separate IoT network for smart devices. You must also implement **2FA** on all core accounts and **disable WPS/UPnP** on the router.

Module 4.3: Counter-Surveillance and Reconnaissance Detection (Physical)

This module teaches you to actively look for signs that a criminal organization is performing **Pre-Operational Surveillance** (reconnaissance) on your home, family, and routines—the critical precursor to a targeted attack.

Identifying Surveillance Indicators (The 'Soft' Indicators)

These are subtle signs that a threat actor is gathering intelligence on your routines.

  • Pattern Disruption: Implement regular, small changes to your routine (e.g., leaving 5 minutes earlier, changing driving routes) to make accurate pre-planning difficult. If your modified routine is observed, it’s a high indicator of surveillance.
  • Unknown Vehicles/Personnel: Keep a mental note of non-local utility vehicles, unmarked vans, or the same unfamiliar vehicle parked multiple times near your home, particularly when family routines occur.
  • Suspicious Maintenance/Solicitation: Be extremely wary of unsolicited or non-scheduled maintenance, delivery, or charity workers who show excessive interest in the home layout, security systems, or family schedules. **Verify all utility work with a call to the company's main line.**

Detection and Documentation Protocol (The 'Hard' Indicators)

These are physical actions taken upon identifying a confirmed surveillance threat.

  1. The "Contact" Confirmation: If you suspect you are being followed, drive a **pre-planned, high-traffic loop** (e.g., past a police station or public camera). If the subject follows the loop, surveillance is confirmed.
  2. Documentation: Use your phone to safely document the threat—capture license plates, vehicle descriptions, and clear photos of the personnel without engaging them directly. Do this only when you are in a safe, public place.
  3. Reporting Protocol: Report all confirmed or highly suspected reconnaissance to the **local police department's non-emergency line** first, providing all documented information. Inform them of the OPSEC measures you are taking to protect your family.

Module Takeaways 4.3

Core Principle: The most important countermeasure is **Pattern Disruption** (changing your routine) to force surveillance teams to reveal themselves. **Always verify unsolicited maintenance** and report confirmed surveillance to the police non-emergency line with detailed documentation (license plate/vehicle).
